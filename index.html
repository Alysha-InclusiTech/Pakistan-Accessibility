POUR — the 4 foundations of accessibility

WCAG looks complex at first. But it all comes down to four principles. Once you understand these, everything else clicks.

P — Perceivable

Everyone should be able to see, hear, or feel your content in some way.

If someone can't see, your content should still reach them another way — through sound or touch. If someone can't hear, captions cover it. The key word: every piece of information must be available in at least one form the user can access.

FAILS WCAG1.4.3 ContrastLevel AARatio: 1.1:1
White on yellow is nearly invisible — both colours are very light. The contrast ratio is only 1.1:1, far below the 4.5:1 minimum. This is one of the most common mistakes on Pakistani government and education websites.
PASSES WCAG1.4.3 ContrastLevel AARatio: 8.6:1
White on blue passes easily — 8.6:1 is nearly double the minimum. This is a reliable, accessible colour combination used widely on accessible government sites.
PASSES WCAG1.4.3 ContrastLevel AARatio: 9.7:1
Dark text on yellow is the fix for yellow backgrounds. This combination passes comfortably. Lesson: yellow backgrounds can work — just pair them with dark text, not white.

FAILS1.1.1 Non-text ContentLevel A
Screen reader announces: "image" — nothing else. A blind user has no idea what this photo shows. Every meaningful image must have a text description. Only decorative images (like background patterns) can skip alt text — and even then, use alt="".
FAILS1.1.1 Non-text ContentLevel A
Screen reader reads: "img zero zero three dot jpg". That's the file name — not useful at all. Alt text should describe what is actually in the image. Your course example is perfect: "Train Railroad tracks with an old looking building on the side."
PASSES1.1.1Level A
Good alt text is 1–2 sentences describing what's in the image. Don't start with "image of" — the screen reader says that already.

People should be able to use your website no matter how they navigate it.

Not everyone uses a mouse. People navigate with keyboards, switch controls, voice commands, or touch screens. Your interface must work with all of them.

FAILS2.5.5 Target SizeLevel AA
Minimum touch target is 44×44px. This button is tiny — anyone on a phone, an older user, or someone with a motor disability will miss it repeatedly. Extremely common on Pakistani banking apps.

An arrow icon tells a sighted user "next" — but a screen reader just says "button". Add visible text or an aria-label: "Next page", "Continue", "Submit". Always describe the action.

FAILS2.1.1 KeyboardLevel A
If pressing Tab doesn't visibly move focus, keyboard-only users are completely stuck. They cannot navigate your site. This is one of the most common failures on custom-built Pakistani government portals.
FAILS2.2.2 Pause Stop HideLevel A
Auto-playing slideshows without a pause button are disorienting for users with attention or cognitive disabilities, and inaccessible for screen reader users. Always add a visible pause control.
PASSES2.1.1 KeyboardLevel A
Full keyboard operability. Tab moves focus, Enter/Space activates. Users who cannot use a mouse can navigate your entire site. This must work for every menu, form, button, and dropdown.
U — Understandable

Your content and instructions should be clear and easy to follow for everyone.

This is about language and behaviour. Use plain language. Make errors helpful, not cryptic. If something behaves unexpectedly, users get confused — even if it technically "works."

CONFUSING3.1.5 Reading LevelLevel AAA
"Authenticate your credentials" is jargon. Many users — especially those with low literacy, dyslexia, or who speak Urdu as their first language — will not know what this means. Simple plain language costs nothing and serves everyone.

"Log in with your username and password" is instantly understood by anyone who can read. This is exactly what your course teaches: accessibility and good UX are the same thing.

FAILS3.3.1 Error IdentificationLevel A
"System Error" tells the user nothing — what went wrong? Which field? What should they do? Users try random things and give up. This is the most common form failure on Pakistani government portals.
PASSES3.3.1 Error IdentificationLevel A
Good error: identifies the exact field, explains the problem, and gives a format example. One clear sentence prevents minutes of frustration — and stops users from abandoning forms entirely.
R — Robust

Your website should work with all kinds of devices and assistive technologies.

A website that works in Chrome on your laptop but breaks on a screen reader isn't accessible. Robust means your code is clean enough that any device or assistive technology can read it correctly.

Testing only on a laptop in Chrome misses the majority of users. In Pakistan, most people access the internet primarily on mobile phones. A site that breaks on mobile fails a huge portion of the population.
GOOD PRACTICE4.1.1 ParsingLevel A

Urdu right-to-left text — Pakistan-specificClick each example

This is one of the most common failures on Pakistani websites. Urdu reads right-to-left — but most sites code it left-to-right by mistake.

❌ Wrong — direction: LTR (left-to-right)
خوش آمدید پاکستان — یہ ایک ٹیسٹ ہے
✓ Correct — direction: RTL (right-to-left)
خوش آمدید پاکستان — یہ ایک ٹیسٹ ہے
FAILS1.3.2 Meaningful SequenceLevel A
Without dir="rtl", Urdu text is forced left-to-right — the reading direction is backwards. Screen readers read it in the wrong order. This breaks almost every Urdu government website in Pakistan.
PASSES1.3.2 Meaningful SequenceLevel A
Two HTML attributes fix this completely: dir="rtl" lang="ur". This tells the browser and screen reader the text flows right-to-left. Every Urdu section of every Pakistani website needs these two attributes.

Why digital accessibility matters in Pakistan

Pakistan has over 27 million persons with disabilities — approximately 12% of the population. As banking, government services, and education move online, inaccessible platforms don't just inconvenience this community — they exclude them entirely from civic and economic life.
